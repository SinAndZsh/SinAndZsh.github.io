A University System Spent Millions on AI.
Now It's Arguing About What Education Is For

Published: July 1, 2026 | Category: Education & Technology | Length: ~1,350 words
文章大意：本文报道了美国最大的公立大学系统——加州州立大学（CSU）耗资1,690万美元与OpenAI签约，为50万师生提供ChatGPT访问权限的事件。文章深入探讨了在预算紧张、学费上涨、教职员工裁员的背景下，巨资投入AI引发的争议：AI究竟是帮助学生学习的工具，还是让学生逃避学习的捷径？支持者认为大学有责任让学生为AI驱动的经济做好准备，批评者则担心学生在掌握基础知识之前就对技术产生依赖。这场争论的核心是一个更宏大的问题：高等教育的使命究竟是职业培训，还是批判性思维与人格塑造？

What is a university supposed/səˈpəʊzd/ adj. 应该的；假定的 to do? That might sound like a strange question. But it sits at the heart of a growing fight across California's public universities, as artificial intelligence forces educators to reconsider the very purpose of higher education.

Last year, the California State University system, which serves hundreds of thousands of students across 22 campuses, signed a lucrative/ˈluːkrətɪv/ adj. 有利可图的；巨额的 $16.9-million deal with OpenAI. The agreement gave students, professors and staff access to half a million ChatGPT licences/ˈlaɪsnsɪz/ n. 许可证；授权（licence的复数） and was promoted/prəˈməʊtɪd/ v. 推广；宣传（promote的过去分词） as a bold step toward preparing graduates for an AI-driven economy.

University leaders saw it as a practical/ˈpræktɪkl/ adj. 实际的；务实的 response to a changing world. In their view, embracing/ɪmˈbreɪsɪŋ/ v. 拥抱；欣然接受（embrace的现在分词） artificial intelligence was not merely an option — it was an imperative/ɪmˈperətɪv/ n. 必要的事；命令 adj. 必要的；紧急的. Many professors and students saw something else entirely, the New York Times reported. To them, the deal raised fundamental/ˌfʌndəˈmentl/ adj. 基本的；根本的 questions about what universities exist to do.

A campus already under pressure

The timing could hardly have been more controversial/ˌkɒntrəˈvɜːʃl/ adj. 有争议的；引起争论的. As AI tools were being rolled out/rəʊld aʊt/ 推出；铺开（roll out的过去分词）, the university system was wrestling with budget problems, tuition increases, faculty layoffs/ˈleɪɒfs/ n. 裁员；解雇（layoff的复数） and the closure of academic programmes. On some campuses, departments were fighting simply to survive. The discrepancy/dɪsˈkrepənsi/ n. 差异；不一致 between cutting essential programmes and spending millions on AI tools struck many as deeply ironic/aɪˈrɒnɪk/ adj. 讽刺的；反讽的.

That created an obvious question. If money was so tight, why was the university spending millions on ChatGPT? The answer, according to administrators/ədˈmɪnɪstreɪtəz/ n. 管理者；行政人员（administrator的复数）, was that the investment would pay off in the long run by making students more employable/ɪmˈplɔɪəbl/ adj. 可雇用的；有就业能力的. Critics were not persuaded.

For critics, the deal became a symbol/ˈsɪmbl/ n. 象征；标志 of what they saw as misplaced priorities/praɪˈɒrətiz/ n. 优先事项；重点（priority的复数）. They worried that universities were rushing to embrace the latest technology while neglecting/nɪˈɡlektɪŋ/ v. 忽视；忽略（neglect的现在分词） the people and programmes that make education possible in the first place. Why invest in chatbots, they asked, when classrooms were understaffed/ˌʌndəˈstɑːft/ adj. 人员不足的 and libraries were cutting hours?

Students caught in the middle

The people most affected by the debate are the students themselves. They find themselves caught between two competing visions/ˈvɪʒnz/ n. 愿景；看法（vision的复数） of what their education should look like.

Walk into one classroom and a professor may encourage students to use AI to brainstorm/ˈbreɪnstɔːm/ v. 头脑风暴；集思广益 ideas, analyse information or improve their writing. In these classrooms, AI is framed/freɪmd/ v. 界定；框定（frame的过去分词） as a powerful tool that can free students from tedious/ˈtiːdiəs/ adj. 乏味的；冗长的 tasks and allow them to focus on higher-order thinking.

Walk into another and the same tools might be banned completely. Professors in these classrooms argue that relying on AI undermines/ˌʌndəˈmaɪnz/ v. 削弱；破坏（undermine的第三人称单数） the very skills students are supposed to be developing. How can you learn to write, they ask, if a machine does it for you?

As a result, many students say they are receiving mixed messages. They are told AI will be essential/ɪˈsenʃl/ adj. 必要的；必不可少的 in the workplace, yet they are also warned about relying on it too heavily. They are encouraged to experiment with new technology while being reminded that it could undermine the very skills they came to university to develop. For many, it feels as though the rules are being written in real time.

Is AI helping students learn or helping them avoid learning?

That is the question that keeps resurfacing/ˌriːˈsɜːfɪsɪŋ/ v. 再次出现；重新浮出水面（resurface的现在分词）. It is a question without easy answers, and it divides even experienced educators.

Supporters argue that ignoring AI would be irresponsible/ˌɪrɪˈspɒnsəbl/ adj. 不负责任的. Whether people like it or not, employers increasingly expect workers to understand these tools. Universities, they say, have a responsibility to prepare students for the world they are entering rather than the world that existed ten years ago. To deprive/dɪˈpraɪv/ v. 剥夺；使丧失 students of AI training, in this view, would be to put them at a disadvantage/ˌdɪsədˈvɑːntɪdʒ/ n. 不利条件；劣势 in the job market.

Critics are not convinced. They worry that students may become dependent/dɪˈpendənt/ adj. 依赖的；依靠的 on technology before they have mastered the fundamentals/ˌfʌndəˈmentlz/ n. 基础；基本原理（fundamental的复数） themselves. If a chatbot can write an outline, summarise a reading or generate ideas, what happens to the process of struggling through a difficult problem and learning from it? The concern is not simply about cheating. It is about whether students are losing opportunities to develop critical thinking, creativity and intellectual independence.

A debate bigger than ChatGPT

What makes the controversy/ˈkɒntrəvɜːsi/ n. 争议；争论 so fascinating is that it is not really about OpenAI. It is about the future of higher education itself.

For decades, universities have balanced two goals. They prepare students for jobs, but they also encourage them to think critically about the world around them. These two missions/ˈmɪʃnz/ n. 使命；任务（mission的复数） have always existed in tension, but they have generally been able to coexist.

Artificial intelligence is forcing universities to reconsider that balance. If AI becomes a routine/ruːˈtiːn/ adj. 常规的；日常的 part of work, then students probably need to understand it. But if universities become too focused on producing workers for the next technological shift, some fear they may lose sight of a broader mission — the cultivation/ˌkʌltɪˈveɪʃn/ n. 培养；教化 of thoughtful, engaged citizens.

A year into California's AI experiment, nobody knows exactly where that line should be drawn. What is clear is that the argument is not going away. The technology is advancing rapidly, universities are continuing to invest in it, and students will have to live with the consequences/ˈkɒnsɪkwənsɪz/ n. 后果；结果（consequence的复数） long after today's debate is over.

长难句解析 Sentence Analysis

Sentence 1: The agreement gave students, professors and staff access to half a million ChatGPT licences and was promoted as a bold step toward preparing graduates for an AI-driven economy.
【参考译文】该协议为学生、教授和工作人员提供了50万个ChatGPT许可证的使用权，并被宣传为让毕业生为AI驱动的经济做好准备的大胆举措。
【结构分析】主句主语为The agreement，后跟两个并列谓语：(1) gave students... access to...（双宾语结构）；(2) was promoted as...（被动语态）。句中介词短语toward preparing graduates for...作后置定语修饰step，其中包含prepare sb. for sth.（使某人为某事做好准备）的固定搭配。
Sentence 2: They worry that students may become dependent on technology before they have mastered the fundamentals themselves.
【参考译文】他们担心，学生在掌握基础知识之前就可能对技术产生依赖。
【结构分析】主句为They worry that...，that引导宾语从句。宾语从句中包含before引导的时间状语从句。"become dependent on"为固定搭配，意为"变得依赖于……"。注意"before"在此处翻译为"在……之前"，体现时间先后关系，也是考研英语中常见的考点。
Sentence 3: What makes the controversy so fascinating is that it is not really about OpenAI — it is about the future of higher education itself.
【参考译文】这场争议之所以如此引人关注，是因为它实际上并非关乎OpenAI，而是关乎高等教育本身的未来。
【结构分析】主句主语为What引导的主语从句（What makes the controversy so fascinating），谓语为is，后接that引导的表语从句。破折号后的内容起补充说明作用，对表语从句的内容进行递进解释。"not...but..."结构（不是……而是……）是考研阅读理解中常见的强调结构。

阅读技巧提示

  • 教育类议论文常采用"引入现象 → 正反对比 → 深层思考"的结构。阅读时注意把握文章的论证脉络，这对解答主旨题至关重要。
  • 注意文中表示对比关系的信号词，如"Supporters argue that... Critics are not convinced..."，这类结构往往是细节题和推理题的出题点。
  • "What makes X so Y is that..."是典型的强调句型，强调的内容（that从句）通常是作者要突出的核心观点。
  • 遇到"not...but..."（不是……而是……）结构时，重点在but之后的内容，这是考研阅读中常见的考点。
  • 推理判断题的答案往往需要整合多方信息，而非直接对应某一句话。正确选项通常是对原文观点的概括和合理延伸。
