Thoughts on AI, From a Prizewinning Writer Accused of Using AI

当获奖作家被指控使用AI：一场关于创作、检测与人性的文学争议

Jamir Nazir贾米尔·纳齐尔（特立尼达作家） has become the face of the AI-writing crisis. In May, the largely unknown 62-year-old Trinidadian writer was named a regional winner of the prestigiousprestigious /preˈstɪdʒəs/ adj. 有声望的；受尊敬的 Commonwealth Prize for his short story "The Serpent in the Grove." Inscrutableinscrutable /ɪnˈskruːtəbl/ adj. 高深莫测的；难以理解的 lines pluckedpluck /plʌk/ v. 摘取；挑选 from Nazir's densedense /dens/ adj. 密集的；难懂的 proseprose /proʊz/ n. 散文 were mockedmock /mɒk/ v. 嘲笑；嘲弄 and memedmeme /miːm/ v. 被制作成网络梗图传播. A young woman in the story "had the kind of walking that made benches become men." Another "smiled like sunrise over a sink." Soon, other winners' stories came under suspicionsuspicion /səˈspɪʃn/ n. 怀疑；嫌疑. The Commonwealth Foundation defended the authors, saying that all had testifiedtestify /ˈtestɪfaɪ/ v. 作证；证明 that their work was original, but it pledged to investigate further.

On Tuesday, the Commonwealth Foundation announced that "The Serpent in the Grove" had been chosen from among the regional winners as this year's overall prize winner. "The team worked hard to understand Jamir's creative process and learn how he shaped his story over time," a spokesperson for the Commonwealth Foundation said. Razmi Farook, the organization's director general, had previously issued a statement on the results of its probeprobe /proʊb/ n. 调查；探究: "After a thorough consultation with our judges and careful consideration of all available information, we are satisfied that AI was not used to write the winning stories." He noted that the investigation did not make use of Pangram or other AI-detection tools, because of their inability to provide conclusiveconclusive /kənˈkluːsɪv/ adj. 结论性的；确凿的 evidence as well as "concerns regarding artistic ownership and consentconsent /kənˈsent/ n. 同意；许可." Instead, the foundation said it had held "detailed discussions" about the regional winners' creative process and examined "working drafts, time-stamped documents and notes" that showed how they developed their stories.

In a phone interview Tuesday afternoon, Nazir said he feels vindicatedvindicated /ˈvɪndɪkeɪtɪd/ adj. 被证明无罪的；得到澄清的—and relievedrelieved /rɪˈliːvd/ adj. 宽慰的；放心的. "Look, I didn't use it!" he said about AI. Now that he has won the prize, Nazir said, he is free at last to explain his process and clear his name. He talked for more than an hour about his writing process, his health, and his views on technology. On several occasions, he seemed to avoid answering questions directly; when he did, some of the answers were circuitouscircuitous /sɜːrˈkjuːɪtəs/ adj. 迂回的；绕圈子的. The interviewer was surprised to hear him opineopine /oʊˈpaɪn/ v. 发表意见；认为 that AI-generated writing will soon be widely accepted in literature, even as he maintained that he didn't use AI tools in creating his story. He seemed bullishbullish /ˈbʊlɪʃ/ adj. 看涨的；乐观的 on AI overall, viewing it as a revolutionary technology, though he worried about the repercussionsrepercussion /ˌriːpərˈkʌʃn/ n. 后果；反响 of saying so.

Nazir explained that his unusual writing style stems fromstem from 源于；来自 physical necessity and literary influences. He was born withbe born with 天生患有 diabetes, and as a result, conventional typing on a keyboard is extremely difficult for him. As he has gotten older, the neuropathyneuropathy /nʊˈrɑːpəθi/ n. 神经病变 has gotten worse, and sitting for long periods at a desk gives him back problems. So he decided that there was no better place to write than the couch, and he found that he could use the speech-to-textspeech-to-text /spiːtʃ tə tekst/ n. 语音转文字（技术） function of the Google keyboard on his Android phone. That function produces words, and then he edits them. Because the phone screen offers only a very small space—about three and a half inches—to see the writing, he looks at those lines, holds the words in his mind, and keeps reviewing and polishingpolish /ˈpɑːlɪʃ/ v. 润色；打磨 them. This process, he said, has given him the opportunity to significantly polish the text. "Why the hell would I need AI?" he asked. "It was absolutely not needed in this story, because I lived this. The story has a lot of real people, real places, and real situations."

To convince the Commonwealth committee that he did not use AI, Nazir produced extensive documentationdocumentation /ˌdɑːkjumenˈteɪʃn/ n. 文件；证明材料. The committee made it clear that producing these materials was voluntaryvoluntary /ˈvɑːlənteri/ adj. 自愿的, because they were not part of the initial rules. But Nazir provided all of the previous drafts and the character profilesprofile /ˈproʊfaɪl/ n. 人物简介；形象 he had sketched outsketch out 草拟；粗略勾画. He also had to sign a statement confirming that no AI was used. The first poet whose work he fell in love with was Pablo Neruda of Chile, and Neruda's teacher Gabriela Mistral. Both had a profoundprofound /prəˈfaʊnd/ adj. 深远的；深刻的 impact on him. Then there is Derek Walcott, a Caribbean poet who, like Pablo Neruda, is a Nobel Prize winner. The thing that attracted Nazir to Walcott was Walcott's complete disregarddisregard /ˌdɪsrɪˈɡɑːrd/ n. 漠视；忽视 for traditional Western sentence structure, which some people cite as proof that Nazir's writing was AI-generated. But Walcott created his own style. Nazir said he looked at how these poets wrote and wrote poetry in their style too.

Nazir also raised a provocative question about AI training. He noted that he has posted significant poems in several poetry groups on Facebook. Once material is on Facebook, AI companies will data-minedata-mine /ˈdeɪtə maɪn/ v. 数据挖掘 it. So they have all of that as a reflection of his style as well. He wondered whether AI companies might attempt to claim his human writing as AI-generated. AI systems are trained on human writing, he suggested, and then when someone puts a human writer's article through an AI detector, the tool may recognize the style and falsely conclude it was generated by an AI system. The interviewer clarified that this is not how a tool like Pangram works; it looks for statisticalstatistical /stəˈtɪstɪkl/ adj. 统计的 patterns in language that are more commonly produced by AI systems than by human writers. Nazir countered by asking why no court of law in any country has accepted AI-detector evidence as admissibleadmissible /ədˈmɪsəbl/ adj. （证据）可被采纳的. "That shows you, in terms ofin terms of 在……方面；就……而言 reliabilityreliability /rɪˌlaɪəˈbɪləti/ n. 可靠性, there's still a lot of refiningrefine /rɪˈfaɪn/ v. 改进；完善 to be done," he said. This case has also called into questioncall into question 对……提出质疑 the reliability of AI-detection tools more broadly.

The conversation also revealed Nazir's complicated views on AI's future. Although he denied using AI for his prize-winning story, he said he had done research with AI for other purposes. He compared the current anxiety about AI to earlier reactions to the typewriter and word processor. When the typewriter was first invented, writers protested that writing should be done with a quill or fountain pen. Later, word processors arrived, and they too were seen as machine assistants because they could check spelling, search, find and replace, and suggest synonyms. Modern word processing can do almost everything, he noted, yet the uproar settled down. He predicted the same would happen with AI. Because he believes AI will eventually be accepted as just another tool, he was asked why he did not simply use it. He replied that he was not saying AI is bad, but because of the current debate, he would not encourage writers in literary competitions to use it for the next two or three years. "I see it as being a tool to be incorporatedincorporate /ɪnˈkɔːrpəreɪt/ v. 纳入；包含 in the future," he said. "A lot of people use it."

Toward the end of the interview, Nazir's memory lapseslapse /læps/ n. 疏忽；记忆衰退 underscored the personal difficulties behind the public controversycontroversy /ˈkɒntrəvɜːrsi/ n. 争议. When asked about his favorite Walcott poem, he could not name a specific one. He explained that he was experiencing brain fog, partly because of his illness and partly because he is on chemotherapy. "I had an excellent memory, and now it haunts me because at times I can't remember even basic stuff," he said. The interview left a complex portrait: a writer trying to prove his humanity to a world increasingly suspicious of machine-generated prose, even as he believes that machines and human creativity may one day coexist.

📝 文章大意

主题：本文报道了特立尼达作家贾米尔·纳齐尔因其短篇小说《林间之蛇》斩获英联邦短篇小说奖而卷入“是否使用AI创作”的争议，最终基金会经调查确认其作品为原创，但事件引发了对AI检测工具可靠性的广泛质疑。

主要内容：62岁的纳齐尔在获选区域获奖者后，其充满诗意且句式奇特的散文被Pangram等AI检测工具标记为“100%人工生成”，社交媒体随之掀起质疑浪潮。英联邦基金会未依赖检测软件，而是通过审阅草稿、时间戳文档与笔记来核实创作过程，并最终授予纳齐尔年度大奖。采访中，纳齐尔解释了自己因糖尿病神经病变而采用手机语音输入、逐行打磨的独特写作方式，并援引沃尔科特、聂鲁达等诗人对自己文风的影响。他还指出，AI模型既然大量学习人类作品，那么将人类独特文风误判为AI生成并非不可能，而迄今没有一国法庭将AI检测结果作为可采纳证据，足以说明其可靠性仍待完善。

核心观点：文章并非简单判定纳齐尔是否使用AI，而是借此争议揭示一个更深层的困境：当AI写作检测成为“疑罪从有”的工具时，边缘地带的作家、非西方句式与独特文学表达更容易被怀疑；技术检测的局限性提醒我们，在算法时代守护“人的声音”需要更审慎的判断，而非对工具的盲目臣服。

Among other indicators, Pangram, an imperfect but industry-leading AI detection tool, flagged the story's text as 100 percent artificial.
主干Pangram flagged the story's text as 100 percent artificial.

修饰成分
• 介词短语：Among other indicators（说明范围）
• 同位语：an imperfect but industry-leading AI detection tool（解释 Pangram 是什么，其中 industry-leading 为复合形容词）

翻译在其他迹象之中，Pangram——一款虽不完美但行业领先的AI检测工具——将这篇小说的文本标记为100%由人工智能生成。
He noted that the investigation did not make use of Pangram or other AI-detection tools, because of their inability to provide conclusive evidence as well as "concerns regarding artistic ownership and consent."
主干He noted that...

修饰成分
• 宾语从句：the investigation did not make use of Pangram or other AI-detection tools
• 原因状语：because of their inability to provide conclusive evidence（说明未使用工具的原因之一）
• 并列原因：as well as "concerns regarding artistic ownership and consent"（第二个原因，regarding 为介词，意为“关于”）

翻译他指出，调查并未使用Pangram或其他AI检测工具，这既是因为它们无法提供确凿证据，也是出于对艺术所有权和（数据）许可的担忧。

主干The thing was Walcott's complete disregard for traditional Western sentence structure.

修饰成分
• 定语从句 1：that attracted Nazir to Walcott（修饰 The thing）
