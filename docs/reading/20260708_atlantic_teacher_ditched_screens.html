What Happened After a Teacher Ditched Screens

一位教师扔掉屏幕之后发生了什么——教育科技的承诺与课堂现实
📰 The Atlantic ✍ Original Article 📅 April 6, 2026 🎯 教育 · 科技

📖 正文精读

For more than a decade, Dylan Kane, a seventh-grade math teacher in Leadville, Colorado, leaned intolean into 投入；积极拥抱 technology as a way to improve his classes. Starting in 2014, he dragged Chromebook carts down hallways to make sure all of his students could use a device. He assessedassess /əˈses/ v. 评估；评定 the available apps, built his own math-instruction website, and embedded software to prevent kids from playing games or exploring inappropriateinappropriate /ˌɪnəˈproʊpriət/ adj. 不适当的；不适宜的 sites. He kept adapters around the room so everyone could charge up.

Then, in January, he took the Chromebooks away. He had sensedsense /sens/ v. 感觉到；意识到 that the technology was falling short of its promises. Within weeks of ditching the screens, he saw how they had been holding both him and his students back. Given my own research on children and education, I reached out to him when I learned from a colleague about his experiment.

Laptops and tablets have become ubiquitousubiquitous /juːˈbɪkwɪtəs/ adj. 无处不在的；普遍存在的 in classrooms across the country. About 90 percent of school districts provide every student with a school-issued laptop or tablet. A nationally representativerepresentative /ˌreprɪˈzentətɪv/ adj. 有代表性的 survey from 2021 of ed-tech use by teachers found that more than half had their students use screens for up to four hours a day, and more than a quarter had students spend at least five hours on screens in a typical school day.

The great promise of educational technology is personalizationpersonalization /ˌpɜːrsənələˈzeɪʃn/ n. 个性化；私人定制. Every student suffers from "Swiss-cheese gaps" in their knowledge, Sal Khan, the founder of Khan Academy and an evangelistevangelist /ɪˈvændʒəlɪst/ n. 热情的倡导者；福音传道者 for how tech and tutoring can enhance learning, once told me. Yet finding and filling these holes is a serious challenge for a teacher with 20 or more students who spanspan /spæn/ v. 跨越；涵盖 several grade levels of ability. Specially designed apps, websites, and now AI tutors touttout /taʊt/ v. 宣扬；吹捧 not just ways to measure the abilities of every student, but also software that adapts toadapt to 适应；根据……调整 each student's interests and skills. Algorithms are meant to ensure that no student falls through the cracksfall through the cracks 被忽略；从缝隙中漏掉.

Yet Kane found that the reality was more complicated. An early adopter, he helped pioneerpioneer /ˌpaɪəˈnɪr/ v. 开创；倡导 an experiment at his school to give eighth graders laptops, and he was a swift cheerleadercheerleader /ˈtʃɪrliːdər/ n. 啦啦队长；热情的支持者 for Desmos, an online graphing tool that lets students drag sliders and manipulatemanipulate /məˈnɪpjuleɪt/ v. 操作；操控 equations. But something was naggingnag /næɡ/ v. 烦扰；使心神不宁 at him. Even with the best-designed software, built with pause functions so he could remotely freeze students' screens to recapturerecapture /ˌriːˈkæptʃər/ v. 重新捕获；重获 their attention, he noticed that his students tended to stare at their screens during class discussions. "There's this gravity that the screens exert on student attention," he told me. "They're waiting for it to unpause, waiting for it to pull them back in."

He tried having them tilttilt /tɪlt/ v. 倾斜；使倾斜 their screens down, but they would slylyslyly /ˈslaɪli/ adv. 偷偷地；狡猾地 tilt them back up. He tried having them close their Chromebooks entirely, but then he was spending precious minutes managing the transitiontransition /trænˈzɪʃn/ n. 过渡；转变 to and from screens, reconnectingreconnect /ˌriːkəˈnekt/ v. 重新连接 to the internet, troubleshooting the inevitable problems: the charger that wouldn't work, the software that inexplicablyinexplicably /ɪnˈeksplɪkəbli/ adv. 无法解释地；难以理解地 blocked the wrong websites, the Chromebook that was suddenly dead. "You might be amazed at how much time I spend dealing with stuff like that," he said.

Already frustrated, Kane read the December book The Digital Delusion, in which Jared Cooney Horvath, a neuroscientist and an educator, blames technology for the widely documenteddocument /ˈdɑːkjument/ v. 记录；记载 decline in student achievement. Although Kane disagreed with Horvath's insistenceinsistence /ɪnˈsɪstəns/ n. 坚持；坚决要求 that screens in classrooms are the primaryprimary /ˈpraɪmeri/ adj. 主要的；首要的 culpritculprit /ˈkʌlprɪt/ n. 罪魁祸首；元凶 behind declining test scores, he began to wonder if the Chromebooks in his own classroom were causing more harm than good.

After years of accumulatedaccumulate /əˈkjuːmjəleɪt/ v. 积累；积聚 doubt, Kane decided to run an experiment: no Chromebooks for a month. He soon discovered that he didn't miss the tech.

Not only did he lose less time futzing overfutz over 瞎忙；浪费时间在琐事上 lost chargers and bad links, but he also noticed right away that his students paid more attention to what he said. Even more striking, Kane found that it was easier to connect with and respond to his students.

"If I walk by Jimmy and I see he answered the first two questions and the third one is blank, that gives me a ton of information," Kane said. When he sees that Jimmy is clearly confused about No. 3, Kane can stop and help him. Screens, however, made it possible for a student to look busy for an entire class period without Kane ever knowing he was stuck. Kane ends every class with a short mixed-practice assignment, a retrievalretrieval /rɪˈtriːvl/ n. 检索；取回 exercise that revisits the day's lesson and older material. He'd been delivering it digitally; in January, he switched to paper. He was surprised to discover that this raised the assignment-completion rate—from 45 to 62 percent—of the students who were the least likely to finish and submit their work before, even though the digital version could technically be finished after class. He now speculatesspeculate /ˈspekjuleɪt/ v. 推测；猜测 that because working in pencil and paper slows students downslow down 放慢速度 and makes their thinking process visible, they are better able to push through hard concepts.

Because Kane's intervention is so recent, his hypothesishypothesis /haɪˈpɑːθəsɪs/ n. 假设；假说 has yet to be confirmed by standardized tests. Over and over again, though, Kane discovered that all of the dashboards and data analyticsanalytics /ˌænəˈlɪtɪks/ n. 分析；数据分析 of ed tech did not make the individual needs of students clearer, nor did they much help those who were struggling. Instead, the screens offered students cover, a way to appear engagedappear engaged 显得专注 without any actual sustainedsustained /səˈsteɪnd/ adj. 持续的；持久的 effort. Only when he got rid of all the laptops and tablets did the needs of his students become plain. "If I walk by and see what they've done on paper, I can respond to their learning," he said.

Education, Kane knows, is profoundly and stubbornlystubbornly /ˈstʌbərnli/ adv. 顽固地；倔强地 social. "There are a lot of students who need accountabilityaccountability /əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/ n. 问责；责任," he said. The answer is not more surveillancesurveillance /sɜːrˈveɪləns/ n. 监视；监督, but more companionshipcompanionship /kəmˈpæniənʃɪp/ n. 陪伴；伙伴关系 in the struggle. "Students benefit from being in a room with a bunch of other people who are learning the same thing, the collective effervescencecollective effervescence 集体亢奋；共同的热烈氛围 of all trying to make progress together," he said. "And they benefit from an adult who knows them, who is in the room, who says 'I care about your learning.'"

Screens, Kane noticed, had made it easier for students—and, if he's being honest, for teachers—to opt out ofopt out of 选择退出；避免参与 that contract. "Chromebooks can be a classroom-management strategy," he said. "Students tend to be a little more dociledocile /ˈdɑːsl/ adj. 温顺的；易驾驭的 with a screen in front of them. And it was just so easy for me to sit behind my screen and watch the little dots marching acrossmarch across 列队行进；逐一走过 the dashboard and not really teach." He's noticed that teaching in an analoganalog /ˈænəlɔːɡ/ adj. 模拟的；非数字的 environment is more demanding. "I'm more fatigued," he said. "But I'm happy with that."

There is no question that technology can support learning. But somewhere along the way, what was once a 30-minute interventionintervention /ˌɪntərˈvenʃn/ n. 干预；介入 became kids glued toglued to 紧盯；全神贯注于 laptops at school—and at home, too, because many homework assignments and materials are virtualvirtual /ˈvɜːrtʃuəl/ adj. 虚拟的；数字的. Educators are still trying to figure out how to balance technological tools that can be both enormously helpful and perniciouslyperniciously /pərˈnɪʃəsli/ adv. 有害地；恶意地 distracting. "I've been in classrooms that had lots of ed tech where very little learning was taking place," Natalie Wexler, the author of Beyond the Science of Reading and The Knowledge Gap, writes, "and I've been in classrooms with very little ed tech where lots of learning was taking place. But the absence of ed tech doesn't ensure learning."

Kane isn't calling for a bonfirebonfire /ˈbɑːnfaɪər/ n. 篝火；火堆 of the Chromebooks. He still believes that technology has genuine value for highly motivated, self-directed learners, and he plans to still use screens occasionally, maybe once a month, for specific tasks. He's even cautiouslycautiously /ˈkɔːʃəsli/ adv. 谨慎地 optimistic about the potential for AI to help teachers generate better classroom materials.

What Kane is skeptical ofbe skeptical of 对……持怀疑态度 is the prevailingprevailing /prɪˈveɪlɪŋ/ adj. 盛行的；普遍的 assumption that technology is a valuable asset for teachers and students in schools. "Classrooms are social spaces and collective endeavorscollective endeavor 集体努力；共同事业," he explained. Screens, however, "tend to distract and divide us."

📝 文章大意

主题：本文通过科罗拉多州七年级数学教师Dylan Kane移除课堂Chromebook的实验，探讨教育科技在课堂中的实际效果与其被过度美化之间的差距。

主要内容：Kane曾是教育科技的积极拥护者，使用Chromebook、在线应用、反游戏软件等工具超过十年。但他逐渐发现，屏幕对学生注意力产生了难以抗拒的“引力”，学生可以在讨论中盯着屏幕，看起来专注实则心不在焉。设备管理、网络故障、充电器丢失等问题也消耗了大量课堂时间。于是他做了一个月的无屏幕实验，结果发现学生更专注、师生互动更顺畅，纸笔作业完成率从45%提升至62%。

核心观点：Kane并不主张彻底抛弃技术，而是质疑“技术对所有学生都有益”的普遍假设。他认为教育的本质是社交性的，需要人与人之间的陪伴与责任，而屏幕往往会分散和割裂这种联系。真正重要的是教师的关注与回应，而非更多的仪表盘与数据分析。

💡 阅读技巧提示

考研英语阅读技巧

  • 例证题识别：本文大量引用Kane的亲身经历和原话，这些例子服务于中心论点——教育科技并非万能。遇到引用人物观点的题目，要判断其是支持还是反驳前文论点。
  • 主旨大意题：注意作者的态度并非全盘否定技术，而是提出“平衡”与“质疑”。选项中若出现绝对化表达如completely、ban all等，通常要小心。
  • 词义猜测题：“Swiss-cheese gaps”“fall through the cracks”“collective effervescence”等表达带有比喻色彩，应结合上下文逻辑推断，而非仅依赖字面意思。
  • 长难句拆分：遇到插入语（如 Sal Khan, the founder...once told me）可先跳过，抓住主干；遇到让步状语（Even with...）要明确其与主句的转折关系。
  • 细节定位法：数字、百分比（如45% to 62%）、书名（The Digital Delusion）是常见考点，阅读时可做标记，便于快速回文定位。
