School Smartphone Bans Seen as 'Punitive' by Young People, Study Says

研究称：学校手机禁令被年轻人视为"惩罚性"措施

📖 正文精读

Across the United Kingdom and an increasing number of countries, policymakers and school leaders have been racing to restrictrestrict /rɪˈstrɪkt/ v. 限制，约束 or ban smartphones in classrooms. The aim is clear: to reduce distractiondistraction /dɪˈstrækʃn/ n. 分心，干扰, improve concentrationconcentration /ˌkɑːnsnˈtreɪʃn/ n. 专注，集中, and protectprotect /prəˈtekt/ v. 保护 young people from the pressurespressure /ˈpreʃər/ n. 压力 of social media during the school day. Yet a new study from University College London (UCL) suggests that outright bans may not be quite as straightforwardstraightforward /ˌstreɪtˈfɔːrwərd/ adj. 简单的，直截了当的 as they seem, and that the voices of young people themselves deserve greater attention.

The study, publishedpublish /ˈpʌblɪʃ/ v. 发表，出版 at the end of June 2026, questioned 732 secondarysecondary /ˈsekənderi/ adj. 中学的；次要的 school students aged 11 to 18, along with 27 teachers and 41 parents. Researchers used questionnairesquestionnaire /ˌkwestʃəˈner/ n. 调查问卷, interviewsinterview /ˈɪntərvjuː/ n./v. 采访；面试, and focus groups to understand how different groups view the growing movement to keep phones out of schools. Their findings revealreveal /rɪˈviːl/ v. 揭示，透露 a sharp generationalgenerational /ˌdʒenəˈreɪʃənl/ adj. 代际的 dividedivide /dɪˈvaɪd/ n. 分歧，差异 that could reshape how educators think about technology policy.

There was widespread agreement among all participants that smartphones can be disruptivedisruptive /dɪsˈrʌptɪv/ adj. 破坏性的，扰乱性的 in the classroom. The constant ping of notificationsnotification /ˌnoʊtɪfɪˈkeɪʃn/ n. 通知, the temptationtemptation /tempˈteɪʃn/ n. 诱惑 to scroll through social media, and the distraction of messaging friends are all familiar problems for teachers trying to hold students' attention. Most adults see a ban as a simple and sensible solution to a problem that has become impossible to ignore.

The numbers tell a striking story. While 87% of teachers and 88% of parents were in favour ofin favour of 支持，赞成 a blanketblanket /ˈblæŋkɪt/ adj. 总括的，全面的 ban on smartphones during the school day, 75% of pupils disagreed with the policy. For young people, the issue looks very different from how adults see it. They do not deny that phones can be distracting; rather, they question whether a total ban is the right way to address the problem.

Students argued that banning smartphones restricted their access to tools they considered essentialessential /ɪˈsenʃl/ adj. 必不可少的 for navigatingnavigate /ˈnævɪɡeɪt/ v. 导航；驾驭 daily life and supporting their learning. They pointed topoint to 指向；指出 practical uses such as checking bus timetablestimetable /ˈtaɪmteɪbl/ n. 时间表, looking up weather forecastsforecast /ˈfɔːrkæst/ n. 预报；预测, and using homework apps. In an age when much of modern life runs through a screen, many teenagers do not see their phones simply as devices for entertainmententertainment /ˌentərˈteɪnmənt/ n. 娱乐. Instead, they view them as organisers, navigators, and learning aids.

Beyond practical tasks, students valued the fact that smartphones offered direct access to helpful support networks. This was especially true for girls, who said their phones helped them feel safer when travelling alone. For some young people, a phone is not just a device for amusement; it is a source of connection, reassurancereassurance /ˌriːəˈʃʊrəns/ n. 安慰，安心, and even protection. The ability to contact family or friends at any moment can reduce anxiety and make public spaces feel less threatening.

The research also uncovered a range of ways that schools are implementingimplement /ˈɪmplɪmənt/ v. 实施，执行 phone bans. Some require students to placeplace /pleɪs/ v. 放置 their phones in specialisedspecialised /ˈspeʃəlaɪzd/ adj. 专门的，专业的, sealed magneticmagnetic /mæɡˈnetɪk/ adj. 磁性的 pouches that remain locked during the school day and are opened by staff at dismissal. Others use designateddesignated /ˈdezɪɡneɪtɪd/ adj. 指定的 lockers or charging stations where devices must be left upon arrival. A few institutions have gone further, mandatingmandate /ˈmændeɪt/ v. 强制执行；授权 that students swap smartphones for basic, non-internet-capable "brick phones" while commuting. Still others rely on hand-in policies, with pupils placing devices in lock-boxes or bins at the start of each day or class period. Most schools also allow discretionarydiscretionary /dɪˈskreʃəneri/ adj. 自由裁量的 exceptionsexception /ɪkˈsepʃn/ n. 例外 for students with medical conditions, special educational needs, or specific safeguarding concerns.

Despite these varied approaches, the researchers warn that simply pushing all phone use until after the school day does not remove the issue from young people's lives. Instead, many students experienced blanket bans as punitivepunitive /ˈpjuːnɪtɪv/ adj. 惩罚性的 - something imposed on them without enough consideration of their needs and perspectives. Such a feeling can undermineundermine /ˌʌndərˈmaɪn/ v. 削弱，破坏 trust between students and school leaders, and may even reduce young people's willingness to engage with wider school rules.

The UCL team suggests that the debate over phones in schools needs a more nuancednuanced /ˈnuːɑːnst/ adj. 微妙的，有细微差别的 approach. Rather than treating all smartphone use as harmful, educators and policymakers might benefit from distinguishing between disruptive use during lessons and legitimatelegitimate /lɪˈdʒɪtɪmət/ adj. 合法的，合理的 uses during breaks or travel. The goal, the researchers argue, should be to help students develop healthy digital habits, rather thanrather than 而不是 simply to remove technology from their hands.

The findings come at a critical moment. Governments in several countries are considering stricter restrictionsrestriction /rɪˈstrɪkʃn/ n. 限制 on young people's access to social media and smartphones. Some politicians have put forward proposalsproposal /prəˈpoʊzl/ n. 提议，建议 for age limits on social media accounts, while others want phones banned from all schools. These proposals respond to real concerns, from cyberbullying and online harassmentharassment /həˈræsmənt/ n. 骚扰 to the mental health pressures created by constant connectivityconnectivity /ˌkɑːnekˈtɪvəti/ n. 连通性.

However, the UCL study is a reminder that the most restrictive option is not always the most effective one. If students feel that rules are unfair, they may find ways to break them rather than to follow them. Understanding how young people actually experience these rules may be the first step toward policies that protect them without making them feel punished.

Digital literacy experts argue that schools have an opportunity to do more than regulate devices. They could teach students how to manage their own screen time, recognise manipulative design features, and use technology in ways that support rather than harm their wellbeing. This approach treats young people as active participants in shaping their digital lives, rather than as passive recipients of adult decisions.

The question of phones in schools is part of a much larger conversation about childhood, technology, and public health. In recent years, researchers have documented rising rates of anxiety and depression among teenagers, and many have pointed to social media as a contributing factor. Parents and teachers who support bans often say they are trying to give children a break from the endless demands of online life.

Yet the UCL researchers caution against assuming that removing phones from school will automatically improve mental health. For some students, especially those who are already isolated or anxious, a phone may be an important link to friends, family, or support services. Taking it away without offering alternatives could do more harm than good.

One recommendation from the study is that schools should involve students in designing phone policies. When young people are asked for their views, they are more likely to understand the reasons behind a rule and to accept it. This does not mean giving students complete control, but it does mean listening to their experiences and taking their concerns seriously.

Another suggestion is that schools should provide clear guidance on when and how phones can be used, rather than relying on vague or arbitraryarbitrary /ˈɑːrbɪtreri/ adj. 任意的，武断的 restrictions. Students who know exactly what is expected of them are less likely to feel that a policy is unfair. At the same time, teachers need training and support to enforce rules consistently and fairly.

In the end, the smartphone is neither purely a tool for learning nor purely a source of harm. Like many technologies, its effects depend on how, when, and why it is used. The UCL study suggests that the most thoughtful school policies will recognise this complexitycomplexity /kəmˈpleksəti/ n. 复杂性 and work with students, rather than against them, to build healthier relationships with the devices that have become central to modern life.

📝 文章大意

主题：本文基于伦敦大学学院（UCL）2026年6月底发布的一项研究，探讨英国学校智能手机禁令引发的代际分歧。

主要内容：研究发现，尽管教师和家长普遍支持在校期间全面禁止使用手机（支持率分别为87%和88%），但75%的学生反对这一政策。学生认为手机是日常生活和学习中不可或缺的工具，可用于查公交时刻表、天气预报和作业应用，同时也是安全感和社交支持的重要来源。研究还介绍了学校实施禁令的多种方式，如磁吸袋、储物柜、"砖块机"替代、上缴政策等，并指出一刀切禁令容易被学生视为"惩罚性"措施，可能损害师生信任。

核心观点：学校手机政策不应简单地全面禁止，而应采取更细致、更具包容性的方法：区分课堂干扰性使用与课间、通勤等合理用途，让学生参与政策制定，帮助他们建立健康的数字使用习惯，而不是把技术从手中夺走。
