Students Are Doing Worse Than You Think

Why universities are failing to teach basic skills — and what it means for the future of education

A growing number of students are entering higher education without mastering the basic literacyn. 读写能力；识字 and numeracyn. 计算能力；算术能力 skills they should have acquired years before. Across OECD countries, nearly 8% of university students perform at the lowest level in reading comprehension, equivalent to what is expected of a ten-year-old child. A nearly identical proportion shows similar deficiencies in mathematics. What is more alarming is that this figure has more than doubled over the past decade, suggesting a sustained decline rather than a temporary blip.

The concerns are not confined to second-tier institutions. Even at elite universities such as Harvard and the University of California, faculty members warn that students arrive with serious gaps in reading and mathematics, forcing professors to review material that should have been mastered in secondary school. In an open letter signed by more than 1,800 mathematics and science professors at the University of California, they expressed unprecedented concern about the deteriorationn. 恶化；退化；堕落 in academic preparation among incoming students.

According to the letter, between 20% and 30% of students enrolled in an introductory calculus course at the Berkeley campus display "severe preparation deficits," to the extent that faculty are compelled to reteach content from secondary education. A separate report from the University of California, San Diego, revealed that the number of first-year students with mathematical skills below the expected high school level has multiplied by nearly thirty in just five years, now representing almost one in eight students. Furthermore, approximately 70% of these students fail to meet the competency level expected of a fourteen-year-old.

These difficulties are not limited to mathematics. Literature professors describe students who are incapable of completing the reading of entire books. According to an internal report at Harvard University, faculty in the humanities and social sciences have opted to shorten required texts because students arrive with less experience in reading complex material, diminished concentration capacity, and reduced ability to sustain attention over extended periods. Texts that were considered standard for students at the same level just ten years ago now present significant challenges.

Although complaints from university faculty are not new, it has been difficult to assess this trend because most countries lack standardized evaluations after secondary school. To overcome this limitation, The Economist analyzed data from the OECD's Survey of Adult Skills, an international assessment conducted approximately every ten years that measures functional abilities in literacy and numeracy among the adult population. The analysis specifically examined results for individuals under 35 who were enrolled in tertiary education.

The findings reveal considerable variability between countries. While the United Kingdom performs at or slightly above the OECD average and has shown some improvement since the previous assessment, the United States ranks among the most concerning. In America, one in seven university students reads at a primary school level, compared to approximately one in twenty a decade ago. In mathematics, the situation is even more troubling: nearly one in five university students performs at the lowest level. Poland and Israel record even higher rates, with 21% and 20% of students respectively reading at a ten-year-old's level.

The analysis points to several contributing factors. The COVID-19 pandemic had a profound impact on schooling, with closures, hybrid systems, and quarantines creating what teachers described as a situation where certain students "never attended secondary school." However, The Economist cautions that the decline in academic performance had already begun before the health crisis. Other factors include increased immigration in some countries, with newly arrived students often coming from more disadvantaged socioeconomic backgrounds, and educational reforms that some critics argue have reduced the emphasis on knowledge acquisition in favor of so-called "cross-cutting competencies."

The growing use of screens has also displaced more enriching activities, particularly reading. OECD data shows that even adults have experienced a decline in reading comprehension, possibly because they read long and complex texts less frequently than in previous decades, reducing the practice necessary to maintain such skills. Adding to these challenges, the rapid adoption of generative artificial intelligence in academic work has introduced a new dimension to the debate. A survey by the Higher Education Policy Institute in Britain found that 94% of university students use AI tools for assessed work, while 12% admit to copying AI-generated text directly.

Universities themselves cannot be considered merely passive observers. For decades, critics have accused higher education institutions of gradually lowering admission requirements to attract more students. With a declining number of eighteen-year-olds in some countries, institutions may be tempted to relax their criteria to avoid losing enrollment. The Economist concludes that universities face a dual challenge: addressing students with growing deficiencies while maintaining academic quality and rigor. Assuming that artificial intelligence will render basic skills in reading, writing, and mathematical reasoning unnecessary is not a pragmatic adaptation, but rather a dangerous risk of lowering expectations for university education.

长难句解析
"According to an internal report at Harvard University, faculty in the humanities and social sciences have opted to shorten required texts because students arrive with less experience in reading complex material, diminished concentration capacity, and reduced ability to sustain attention over extended periods."
结构分析
主语：faculty（大学教师）
谓语：have opted to shorten（选择缩短）
宾语：required texts（指定阅读材料）
原因状语从句：because students arrive with...（因为学生缺乏……）
with后接三个并列名词短语：less experience... / diminished concentration... / reduced ability...

参考译文
根据哈佛大学的一份内部报告，人文与社会科学领域的教师们选择缩短指定阅读材料的长度，因为学生们阅读复杂文本的经验不足，注意力集中能力下降，且在较长时间内保持专注的能力也减弱了。
"Assuming that artificial intelligence will render basic skills in reading, writing, and mathematical reasoning unnecessary is not a pragmatic adaptation to the new times, but rather a dangerous risk of lowering expectations for university education."
结构分析
主语：Assuming that...（动名词短语作主语，其中that引导宾语从句）
系动词：is not... but rather...（不是……而是……）
表语1：a pragmatic adaptation（务实的适应）
表语2：a dangerous risk（危险的风险）

参考译文
认为人工智能将使阅读、写作和数学推理等基本技能变得不再必要，这并不是对新时代的务实适应，而是降低大学教育期望的危险冒险。
"What is more alarming is that this figure has more than doubled over the past decade, suggesting a sustained decline rather than a temporary blip."
结构分析
主语从句：What is more alarming（更令人担忧的是）
系动词：is
表语从句：that this figure has more than doubled...
伴随状语：suggesting...（现在分词短语，表示结果/伴随）
rather than：而不是（对比结构）

参考译文
更令人担忧的是，这一数字在过去十年中翻了一番多，这表明这是一种持续的下滑，而非暂时的波动。

阅读理解练习
1. 【主旨大意题】What is the main idea of this article?
A. The COVID-19 pandemic has caused permanent damage to global education systems.
B. University students in developed countries are increasingly lacking basic literacy and numeracy skills, and universities must address this challenge without lowering standards.
C. Artificial intelligence is replacing traditional education and making basic skills obsolete.
D. Elite universities like Harvard and UC Berkeley should raise their admission requirements.
正确答案：B
解析：本文开篇即指出大学生读写和计算能力严重下滑的现象，随后用OECD数据、多所大学教授的反馈等论据展开分析，最后呼吁大学不应以降低标准来应对这一挑战。A选项过于片面（疫情只是原因之一）；C选项与文章观点相反；D选项不是文章主旨。
2. 【细节理解题】According to the article, what percentage of UC Berkeley students in introductory calculus courses show "severe preparation deficits"?
A. 8%
B. 12%
C. 20-30%
D. 70%
正确答案：C
解析：根据文章第三段，"between 20% and 30% of students enrolled in an introductory calculus course at the Berkeley campus display 'severe preparation deficits'"（伯克利分校选修微积分入门课程的学生中，有20%至30%表现出"严重的准备不足"）。注意区分：8%是OECD国家大学生阅读最低水平的平均比例；12%是UC San Diego数学不达标新生的比例；70%是这些学生未能达到14岁预期水平的比例。
3. 【推理判断题】What can be inferred about the author's attitude toward using AI in university education?
A. The author believes AI will completely transform higher education for the better.
B. The author is concerned that AI may be used as an excuse to lower academic standards.
C. The author thinks AI should be banned from all academic work.
D. The author is neutral and presents only facts without any opinion.
正确答案：B
解析：文章最后一段明确指出，"Assuming that artificial intelligence will render basic skills... unnecessary is not a pragmatic adaptation... but rather a dangerous risk of lowering expectations"（认为AI将使基本技能变得不再必要，并不是务实的适应，而是降低期望的危险冒险）。这表明作者担忧AI被用作降低学术标准的借口。A与作者观点相反；C过于极端（作者并未呼吁禁止AI）；D不正确，因为作者明确表达了自己的观点。

阅读技巧提示
🎯 技巧一：把握数据类文章的论证结构
本文属于典型的"现象—数据—原因—结论"结构。阅读时建议先快速浏览首尾段把握主旨，再关注文中的数字和对比（如8%、 doubled、 one in seven等），这些数据往往是出题点。
🎯 技巧二：识别同义替换
考研阅读中，正确选项往往是对原文的同义改写。如本文中"deterioration"（恶化）可与"decline""deficit""worsening"互换；"compel"（迫使）与"force""oblige"意思相近。积累同义词是提升正确率的关键。
🎯 技巧三：长难句抓主干
遇到长难句时，先找谓语动词确定句子主干，再分析修饰成分。如本文最后一句的主干是"Assuming... is not a pragmatic adaptation... but rather a dangerous risk"，理解了这个框架，整句意思就清晰了。
🎯 技巧四：区分事实与观点
本文中OECD数据、大学教授人数等属于客观事实；而"dangerous risk""alarming"等则表达了作者的观点态度。考研推理题经常考查作者态度，需要敏锐识别表达态度的形容词和副词。
